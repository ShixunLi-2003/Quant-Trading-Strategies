# DMR-MFA Attribution Analysis Summary

## Scope of Analysis

This document is a consolidated attribution summary based on all materials in the `Attribution Analysis` folder, covering:

- Performance overview and risk statistics
- Cumulative return, logarithmic equity curve, annual return, and monthly return distribution
- Position-level profit and loss details and top stock contributors
- Brinson industry attribution breakdown
- Industry allocation, style factor exposure, and risk factor exposure
- Position statistics, trading statistics, drawdown ranges, turnover, and intraday return behavior
- Rolling Beta, rolling Sharpe, and slippage sensitivity analysis

The overall conclusion is that `DMR-MFA` is a high-conviction Alpha strategy with very strong long-term return convexity, clear active exposure, and a pronounced stock selection and trading-driven return profile. Its excess return is not primarily generated by simple industry rotation alone, but more by the combined effect of directional judgment, stock/style exposure, and execution.

3. The return distribution is positively skewed and fat-tailed, meaning large winning periods contribute meaningfully to final net value, while the path itself is not low-volatility or uniformly smooth.
4. Beta is below 1 but not low, which means the strategy still carries meaningful market sensitivity. It should be viewed as an Alpha strategy with active exposure rather than a market-neutral or purely low-correlation return source.

## II. Return Path and Regime Characteristics

From the cumulative return curve and the logarithmic equity curve, the strategy shows a clear “early accumulation, later acceleration” structure from `2011-01-04` to `2026-04-24`:

- `2011-2014`: Equity growth was relatively flat, representing a capability-building and signal-calibration phase.
- `2015`: A highly significant step-up in annual return appeared, marking the first major profit expansion phase.
- `2016-2020`: The strategy maintained positive growth, although at a more moderate pace.
- After `2021`: The slope of the return curve increased materially, and equity convexity became much stronger, indicating a clear release of the strategy’s edge.
- `2025-2026`: The strategy remained at a high return plateau, but with visibly larger net value fluctuations and deeper pullbacks.

The logarithmic equity curve is especially important because it shows that even after adjusting for the visual amplification of later-stage gains, the long-term trend remains very clear. This suggests that compounding was not driven only by a handful of isolated windfall periods, but by a persistent accumulation process.

## III. Annual and Monthly Return Distribution

The annual return chart suggests that performance was not released evenly every year, but instead displayed strong regime dependence:

- `2015` was the strongest year, with return close to `400%`
- `2020-2022` formed the second major strong-performance cycle
- `2025` was another clearly high-return year
- A few years were near flat, indicating periodic consolidation phases

The monthly return series and heatmap show the following:

- The monthly return distribution is clearly right-skewed, with many positive months above `10%` and some above `20%`.
- Negative months are generally contained, though there is still a meaningful share of moderate drawdown months.
- The most extreme positive month appears near `40%`, while the weakest months are roughly in the `-10%` to `-12%` range.
- The strategy does not depend on a single isolated explosive month. Instead, high-return months reappear across multiple cycles and market environments.

This implies that the strategy has strong upside participation, but also that its returns include trend amplification or high-beta sector/stock expansion rather than being a purely slow, low-volatility mean-reversion engine.

## IV. Brinson Attribution Conclusions

The Brinson breakdown chart reports:

- Excess returns: `85634.10%`
- Active allocation: `-4180.33%`
- Target selection: `-8655.89%`
- Interaction effect: `98470.32%`

This set of results is highly informative. It indicates that although excess return is very large, it was not generated by industry allocation alone or stock selection alone in a stable standalone way. Instead:

1. Active allocation is negative, which suggests that pure sector weight deviation versus the benchmark did not consistently add value by itself.
2. Target selection is also negative, indicating that if stock selection is isolated within sectors, it was not independently a stable positive contributor.
3. The dominant source of excess return is the very strong interaction effect, meaning the strategy benefited most from simultaneously holding the right stocks within the right industries at the right times.

This means the edge of `DMR-MFA` is better described as a dynamic environment-sensitive composite Alpha rather than a static allocation model. Industry, style, flow, and price-action signals appear to be strongly coupled, with much stronger performance jointly than separately.

## V. Industry Allocation and Sector-Level Characteristics

The industry allocation comparison shows a clear deviation from the benchmark:

- Cash weight is approximately `45.82%`
- Strong underweight in “Others” and financials
- Relative overweight in industrials, information technology, and raw materials
- Low or nearly absent exposure to consumer staples, healthcare, and real estate

This supports three important observations:

1. The strategy is not continuously fully invested. It clearly includes a timing-aware exposure control or risk contraction mechanism.
2. Portfolio construction leans toward sectors with higher earnings elasticity and stronger trading elasticity rather than traditional defensive or stable-weight sectors.
3. Excess return is more likely driven by structural opportunities in high-beta manufacturing, technology, and cyclical materials.

From an attribution perspective, this is fully consistent with a “dynamic mean reversion + multi-factor flow” framework: the strategy is more willing to take risk in sectors where price dislocation, mean-reversion potential, and flow-driven elasticity are aligned.

## VI. Style Exposure and Risk Exposure

### 1. Style Factors

The style analysis chart tracks exposure to `SMB`, `HML`, `RMW`, and `CMA`. Broadly speaking:

- `SMB` is positive for much of the sample, indicating a persistent small/mid-cap bias.
- `HML` is often negative, suggesting the portfolio is not a traditional deep-value strategy and is more aligned with growth or elasticity-oriented pricing.
- `RMW` is generally negative, which means the strategy is not simply harvesting highly profitable, high-quality firms.
- `CMA` fluctuates substantially, indicating that investment-intensity exposure changes meaningfully across market regimes.

### 2. Risk Factors

The risk exposure chart shows the following notable differences:

- Positive nonlinear market cap exposure and negative market capitalization exposure: consistent with a structural tilt toward smaller and more elastic stocks
- Beta exposure around `0.52`, about `0.51` above the benchmark difference line
- Residual volatility exposure around `0.38`, clearly positive
- Liquidity exposure around `0.40`, clearly positive
- Momentum exposure around `0.18`, moderately positive
- Negative exposure to book-to-market, profitability, and leverage

This means the strategy’s return engine is closely tied to a profile of high liquidity, high elasticity, smaller capitalization, and relatively high volatility. In other words, this is not a conservative stock-picking framework. It deliberately embraces faster price response and more active trading conditions.

## X. Turnover, Intraday Return Behavior, and Slippage Sensitivity

### 1. Daily traded shares and turnover

The daily traded shares scatter plot shows that trading size expanded significantly after `2021`, with especially high activity during `2024-2026`. The daily turnover plot shows many observations near `0.5`, alongside many low-turnover and near-flat-exposure days.

This implies:

- The strategy materially increases trading intensity during stronger opportunity regimes.
- Exposure and turnover are not generated in a stable linear fashion, but instead switch clearly across different states.
- Flow and market activity are likely important conditions for return realization.

### 2. Intraday returns

The intraday return series fluctuates around zero, but with sharp spikes on both the upside and downside. This indicates that the strategy is sensitive to short-term price shocks and that part of the return release process is closely related to intraday volatility.

### 3. Slippage impact

The slippage sensitivity chart shows:

- Under `2bp`, `4bp`, and `8bp`, the equity curve remains extremely strong
- At `16bp`, performance drops materially
- At `32bp`, cumulative return remains positive, but is heavily compressed

Therefore, while the strategy has enough Alpha to absorb normal execution costs, it is not immune to high-slippage environments. If capital scale continues to expand, execution cost will become an increasingly important constraint on realized attribution quality.

Combined with the rolling Sharpe and rolling Beta charts:

- The 6-month and 12-month rolling Beta are usually between `0.5` and `1.0`, with occasional spikes into the `1.2-1.6` range
- Rolling Sharpe rises sharply in strong periods and falls significantly, sometimes below zero, in weaker periods

This shows that the strategy has a strong state-dependent nature: when the environment matches its signal structure, return efficiency becomes very high; when the environment does not match, performance volatility rises materially.

## XII. Integrated Assessment

Across all attribution materials, the core strengths of `DMR-MFA` can be summarized as follows:

1. Exceptionally strong long-term compounding, with performance not fully explained by benchmark Beta.
2. Excess return is primarily generated by interaction effects, special returns, and stock/industry alignment in high-elasticity phases rather than by a single static exposure dimension.
3. The portfolio is clearly tilted toward smaller-cap, more liquid, more volatile, and more growth- or elasticity-oriented stocks.
4. The return realization mechanism is closer to a composite active trading system built on dynamic exposure adjustment, event/trend amplification, and mean-reversion capture.

At the same time, the strategy has several constraints that require continuous attention:

1. Return concentration is high, with top names contributing a large share of total gains.
2. Strong return potential comes with medium-to-high volatility and a non-trivial drawdown profile.
3. The strategy is sensitive to trading activity and execution cost, so slippage erosion must be carefully monitored as capacity scales.
4. Industry allocation and stock selection do not look consistently strong when isolated. The real edge emerges from multi-dimensional coupling, which means signal decay may not first appear in a single standalone metric.

## Conclusion

`DMR-MFA` is a distinctly active, high-elasticity Alpha strategy. Its attribution profile shows that the most valuable part of the process is not simply taking exposure to one sector, one style, or one generic factor. Instead, its core strength lies in combining exposure control, industry timing, stock selection, liquidity conditions, and price behavior into a coherent return engine during favorable market states.

If the strategy is to be improved further, the most important optimization directions are likely:

- Reducing excessive concentration risk in a handful of names or local time windows
- Controlling drawdown during high-volatility and high-Beta phases
- Improving transaction cost efficiency without damaging the core Alpha structure

These directions would make it more likely that the strategy’s already impressive return-generating ability can be translated into a more stable and scalable live implementation.
